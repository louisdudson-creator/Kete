Grounding: coming back to the present

What this is for: grounding can help when you feel panicky, overwhelmed, numb, disconnected, triggered, or pulled into frightening thoughts, memories, voices, or body sensations.

When not to rely on this alone: use urgent support if you feel unable to stay safe, are at risk of harming yourself or someone else, or feel so disconnected that you cannot orient to where you are.

Why this helps

Grounding is not about forcing yourself to calm down. It is a way of helping your brain notice, “I am here, now.” This can give the thinking part of the brain more room to return.

Try this step by step

  • Orient: keep your eyes open if that feels safer. Look around and name where you are, the date, and one thing that tells you this is the present.
  • Feet and contact: press your feet into the floor. Notice the chair, wall, clothing, or object touching your body.
  • 5–4–3–2–1: name five things you can see, four you can feel, three you can hear, two you can smell, and one you can taste.
  • Sensory anchor: hold something textured, sip cold water, wrap in a blanket, notice a scent, or use safe movement such as stretching your hands.
  • Safety statement: say, “I am here. This is today. I am in this room. I can take one next step.”

Other grounding options

  • Mental grounding: name categories, count slowly, read words backwards, or describe an ordinary task in detail.
  • Physical grounding: run cool water over your hands, grip the chair, touch different textures, or notice your feet.
  • Soothing grounding: use a blanket, safe scent, music, warm drink, or kind phrase.

If this does not work straight away

Grounding can feel too simple or may need repeating. Try changing the sense you use: visual grounding, sound, touch, temperature, movement, or connection with another person. Practise when calm so it is easier to use when distressed.

Try this now: look around and name three colours, three shapes, and three sounds.

Safety note: some mindfulness or body-focused exercises can increase distress for people with trauma, dissociation, panic, or psychosis vulnerability. Use eyes-open, external, sensory grounding if internal focus feels unsafe.

Panic support: riding out the alarm wave

What this is for: sudden waves of panic, racing heart, breathlessness, shaking, dizziness, chest tightness, nausea, fear of losing control, or feeling that something awful is about to happen.

When not to rely on this alone: seek medical or urgent help if symptoms are new, severe, different from usual, linked with chest pain, fainting, injury, overdose, substance use, or if safety feels uncertain.

Why this helps

Panic is a strong alarm response. The sensations are real and frightening, but panic usually rises, peaks, and falls like a wave. Fighting the sensations or repeatedly checking them can keep the alarm switched on.

Try this step by step

  • Name it: “This feels like panic or an alarm response.” You do not need to be completely certain.
  • Orient: keep your eyes open, look around the room, and name three neutral objects.
  • Unclench: lower your shoulders, loosen your jaw, and let your hands open if possible.
  • Lengthen the out-breath: breathe in normally, then make the out-breath a little longer. Do not force deep breathing if that makes panic worse, and do not use paper-bag breathing.
  • Describe sensations neutrally: “fast heart,” “tight chest,” “warm face,” “shaky legs.” These are body sensations, not instructions.
  • Choose one safe action: sit, stand, walk slowly, sip water, text someone, or return gently to the task if safe.

Avoid paper-bag breathing: it is not recommended and can be unsafe. Use slower, gentler breathing, orientation, movement, or support instead.

After the panic wave

Notice what helped even slightly. Avoid judging yourself for having panic. If you begin avoiding more places or activities, consider gradual exposure with support. Repeated safe practice can help the brain relearn that discomfort can be survived.

Try this now: breathe out slowly once, place both feet on the floor, and name the room you are in.

Safety note: panic skills support coping; they do not replace medical assessment when symptoms are unusual, severe, or medically concerning.

ACT defusion: unhooking from sticky thoughts

What this is for: repetitive, cruel, frightening, shame-based, or bossy thoughts that feel fused with reality, such as “I can’t cope,” “I’m useless,” or “something bad will happen.”

When not to rely on this alone: use support if thoughts are linked with self-harm, suicide, command voices, escalating paranoia, mania, trauma flooding, or feeling unable to stay safe.

Why this helps

Defusion is an ACT skill. It does not try to prove a thought wrong or replace it with positive thinking. It helps you notice a thought as something your mind is doing, so the thought has less power to push you around.

Try this step by step

  • Notice: “A thought is showing up.”
  • Name it: “I’m having the thought that…”
  • Create more space: “I’m noticing I’m having the thought that…”
  • Thank your mind: “Thanks mind, I can see you are trying to protect me.”
  • Use a metaphor: imagine the thought as a radio playing in the background, a leaf floating past, or words on a screen. You do not have to turn the thought off before doing something useful.
  • Try a playful option: say the thought in a cartoon voice or sing it briefly. This is not to mock pain; it is to loosen the grip of the words.
  • Ask the workability question: “If I let this thought run my behaviour, does it move me toward or away from the person I want to be?”
  • Choose a values step: one small action linked with care, courage, health, whānau, learning, honesty, or connection.

If this feels fake

Defusion often feels awkward at first. The thought may still feel true. The aim is not to feel better instantly; it is to loosen the grip enough to choose one useful action.

Try this now: write one sticky thought, then put “I’m noticing I’m having the thought that…” in front of it.

Safety note: if a thought involves immediate harm, do not treat it as a private exercise only. Move toward safety and contact support.

Behavioural activation: small actions before motivation

What this is for: low mood, withdrawal, low motivation, loss of routine, reduced pleasure, feeling stuck, or waiting until you “feel ready” before doing anything.

When not to rely on this alone: use professional or urgent support if depression is severe, risk is increasing, you cannot care for basic needs, sleep has changed drastically, or hopelessness feels unsafe.

Why this helps

Depression often reduces activity. Then less activity reduces mood, confidence, energy, connection, and routine. Behavioural activation gently reverses this cycle. Motivation often comes after action, not before it.

Choose one activity type

  • Pleasure: music, warm drink, sunlight, nature, humour, a favourite show, a small hobby.
  • Mastery: shower, dishes for two minutes, one email, medication routine, opening curtains, paying one bill.
  • Connection: text someone, sit near whānau, go to a group, greet a neighbour.
  • Values: one tiny action that reflects the kind of person you want to be.

Plan it so it can happen

  • Make it specific: what, when, where, and for how long.
  • Use the low-energy version first: “walk to the letterbox” rather than “go for a run.”
  • Track before/after mood or energy from 0–10, without using the number to criticise yourself.
  • Repeat small actions. Improvement usually comes from patterns, not one perfect day.

If this does not work

The task may be too big, too vague, or not connected to what matters. Shrink it by 90%, ask for support, or choose a connection task rather than a productivity task.

Try this now: choose one five-minute action for today: open curtains, drink water, step outside, put three things away, or message one safe person.

Distress tolerance: getting through the wave safely

What this is for: emotional distress that is too intense for problem-solving, including urges to lash out, shut down, self-harm, use substances, send unsafe messages, or make big decisions while flooded.

When not to rely on this alone: if you may harm yourself or someone else, have taken an overdose, cannot stay safe, or voices are commanding harm, move toward urgent support. In New Zealand call 111 for immediate danger or call/text 1737 for crisis support.

Why this helps

Distress tolerance is crisis survival. It is not about fixing your whole life in the middle of a wave. The first aim is to lower intensity and prevent extra harm. When distress is very high, body-based and safety-based skills usually come before insight, analysis, or problem-solving.

Use the intensity ladder

IntensityBest first move
8–10Body and safety first: cool water/cool pack if safe, safe movement, longer out-breath, move away from means of harm, contact support.
6–8Strategic distraction, grounding, soothing, support contact, or a short time container.
4–6Make room for the feeling, defusion, urge surfing, thought record, or a small problem-solving step.
0–4Review what happened, plan prevention, repair if needed, update your crisis plan, practise values-based action.

Try this step by step

  • Pause the decision: delay major actions, messages, spending, substances, driving, or confrontations until the wave drops.
  • Use biology first: splash cool water, use safe movement, then lengthen the out-breath. Adapt for your body and medical needs.
  • Use a time container: set 10–20 minutes and do one neutral task. You are setting the problem down briefly, not abandoning it.
  • Ground: hold an object, name what you can see, feel your feet, and describe the room in detail.
  • Make room: when intensity drops, name the emotion and where it sits in the body without trying to force it away.
  • Choose the next safe step: support person, crisis plan, rest, food/water, medication as prescribed, or one small repair.

Distress ladder: choosing the right skill first

What this is for: moments where you have many coping tools but cannot work out which one to use.

When not to rely on this alone: if risk is immediate, do not spend time choosing the perfect skill. Move toward another person, crisis support, 1737, emergency care, or 111.

The key idea

Do not ask a flooded brain to do complex reflection. Start with the simplest intervention that matches the intensity. As distress drops, move from body-based tools to attention tools, then to meaning, problem-solving, and prevention.

1. Full alarm
Use cold cue if safe, safe movement, longer out-breath, move away from danger, and contact support.
2. Too loud to think
Use strategic distraction: a timed neutral task that uses attention, such as sorting, dishes, walking, craft, puzzle, or categories.
3. Mind racing
Use grounding: grip an object, describe the room, feel feet on the floor, name date/place/time, and label body sensations.
4. Feeling is present but workable
Use defusion, making room, thought record, values step, problem-solving, or repair.

Common trap

Trying to analyse the whole problem at 9/10 often increases distress. Stabilisation first is not avoidance; it is sequencing.

Sleep reset: rebuilding the sleep signal

What this is for: irregular sleep, trouble falling asleep, waking often, lying awake worrying, sleeping late, or sleep problems that worsen mood, anxiety, concentration, irritability, or voices/paranoia vulnerability.

When not to rely on this alone: seek medical or mental health support for severe persistent insomnia, suspected sleep apnoea, restless legs, medication side effects, trauma nightmares, substance withdrawal, or reduced need for sleep with unusually high energy, racing thoughts, impulsivity, or elevated mood.

Why this helps

Sleep and distress feed each other. Poor sleep increases emotional reactivity and makes coping skills harder to access; distress then makes sleep harder. CBT-I-informed sleep support works by rebuilding repeated cues: wake time, daylight, reduced stimulation, bed as a sleep cue, and a calmer response to being awake.

Start with pattern, not pressure

Not every sleep problem is the same. Before adding rules, notice the pattern: trouble falling asleep, waking often, waking too early, sleeping at the wrong time, nightmares, breathing pauses/snoring, restless legs, medicines/substances, pain, trauma reminders, or reduced need for sleep with high energy. The pattern helps decide the safest next step.

Try this step by step

  • Anchor wake time: choose a realistic wake time and keep it as steady as possible for several days. This is usually more useful than forcing an early bedtime.
  • Use light: get daylight early in the day where possible. Open curtains, step outside, or sit near a bright window.
  • Protect the evening: reduce late caffeine, nicotine/vaping, alcohol, bright screens, gaming, heated conversations, and clock-watching if they affect you.
  • Wind down predictably: create a 20–40 minute routine that lowers stimulation: dim lights, prepare tomorrow, shower, gentle stretching, reading, calming audio, or a short relaxation practice.
  • Park worries before bed: write worries and one next step earlier in the evening. Bed is not the place for problem-solving if you can help it.
  • Rebuild bed as a sleep cue: keep bed mainly for sleep, rest, and intimacy. Avoid long periods of scrolling, arguing, working, or analysing distress in bed.
  • If awake and frustrated: turn the clock away. Tell yourself: “I am resting even if I am not sleeping.” If you are awake for a long time and wound up, get up briefly if practical and do something quiet, dim, and boring. Return when sleepy.

Sleep hygiene: small habits that support sleep

What this is for: making sleep easier by adjusting daily habits, bedroom cues, light, caffeine, screens, naps, and wind-down routines.

When not to rely on this alone: seek health or mental health advice for severe or persistent insomnia, suspected sleep apnoea, restless legs, trauma nightmares, substance withdrawal, medication side effects, or reduced need for sleep with high energy, racing thoughts, impulsivity, or elevated mood.

Why this helps

Sleep hygiene means the ordinary habits and environment that tell the brain and body when to be alert and when to wind down. It does not force sleep. It makes sleep more likely by repeating steady cues.

Choose one or two changes first

  • Morning anchor: keep wake time reasonably consistent and get daylight early where possible.
  • Caffeine timing: notice whether coffee, energy drinks, tea, cola, nicotine, or vaping affect your sleep, especially later in the day.
  • Sleep space: make the room as cool, dark, quiet, and comfortable as possible. Turn clocks away if checking the time fuels stress.
  • Wind-down: use a short repeatable routine: dim lights, prepare tomorrow, shower, reading, calm audio, karakia/prayer if meaningful, or gentle stretching.
  • Bed as a sleep cue: avoid using bed for long scrolling, work, conflict, or problem-solving. If awake and frustrated for a long time, get up briefly and do something quiet and dim until sleepy.
  • Naps and catch-up sleep: if naps make night sleep worse, shorten them or move them earlier. Avoid using long daytime sleep as the main fix for poor nights unless advised.

If this does not work

Do not keep adding more rules. Sleep pressure often gets worse when sleep becomes a performance test. Track patterns for a week and take the information to a GP, prescriber, pharmacist, therapist, or mental health team if sleep remains difficult.

Try this now: choose one sleep hygiene experiment for the next three nights: same wake time, morning light, caffeine cut-off, clock turned away, or a 15-minute wind-down routine.

Night distress: when everything feels worse at 2am

What this is for: night-time worry, shame, fear, trauma reminders, voices, loneliness, nightmares, or catastrophic thoughts that become stronger when you are tired and trying to sleep.

When not to rely on this alone: if you feel unable to stay safe, might act on self-harm or suicidal thoughts, or voices are commanding harm, contact urgent support. In New Zealand call 111 for immediate danger or call/text 1737 for support.

Why night makes distress louder

At night there are fewer distractions, less social contact, more quiet, and more fatigue. A tired brain has less access to perspective and problem-solving. The thought may feel certain, but it may be being judged by an exhausted threat system.

Night plan

  • Do not debate your whole life in bed. Write one line: “This is a night-time thought. I will review it tomorrow at ___.”
  • Remove the clock. Watching time pass usually adds pressure and arousal.
  • Use gentle settling, not intensity. Try a longer out-breath, relaxing jaw/shoulders, warm non-caffeinated drink, soft audio, prayer/karakia if this fits you, or a grounding object.
  • If wound up, leave bed briefly if practical. Do something dim, boring, and safe. Return when sleepy. Avoid bright screens and big decisions.
  • Park the problem. Write one next step for tomorrow: call, email, appointment, support person, or task.
  • If safety is uncertain, do not stay alone with it. Contact a person, crisis line, 1737, emergency service, or 111 depending on risk.

If you wake from a nightmare or flashback

  • Orient first: turn on a soft light if needed, name the room, date, and one sign that you are in the present.
  • Use a body anchor: feet on the floor, hands around a mug, cool water, a blanket, or a textured object.
  • Do not analyse the dream straight away: write one neutral line if needed, then return to grounding or a low-stimulation activity.
  • If nightmares repeat: consider talking with a clinician. Nightmare-focused work, imagery rehearsal/rescripting, trauma therapy, medicine review, or sleep assessment may be appropriate, but it is best done with support if the images are trauma-linked or overwhelming.

Crisis plan: what to do when things get unsafe

What this is for: times when distress, suicidal thoughts, self-harm urges, voices, paranoia, substance use, hopelessness, agitation, or exhaustion may make it harder to think clearly.

When not to rely on this alone: if there is immediate danger in New Zealand, call 111 now or go to the nearest emergency department. If you need urgent mental health support, call or text 1737. A plan is a tool; it is not a substitute for emergency or clinical care.

Fill this out when you are calmer

  • My warning signs: changes in sleep, eating, isolation, agitation, giving up, unsafe urges, intense shame, voices becoming threatening, paranoia increasing, substance use, or feeling trapped.
  • My triggers or pressure points: conflict, grief, anniversaries, substances, trauma reminders, money pressure, pain, loneliness, lack of sleep, or feeling rejected.
  • My reasons to stay safe: people, pets, whānau, values, future moments, faith, culture, responsibilities, unfinished hopes, or “I do not have to decide forever today.”
  • Internal coping strategies: grounding, cold water, safe movement, music, distraction, breathing space, shower, blanket, step outside, simple routine.
  • People and places that help: safe whānau, friends, neighbours, peer support, marae/community supports, public places, clinician, GP, crisis team.
  • Professional supports: GP, key worker, mental health team, crisis team, after-hours service, 1737, Lifeline, Youthline, emergency department, 111.
  • Environmental safety steps: move away from means of harm, ask someone to hold medication or sharp objects if appropriate, avoid alcohol/drugs, stay near safe people, do not drive if unsafe.

If risk escalates

Do not wait to be “sure enough.” Use the next level of support early. Ask someone to stay with you, go to a safer place, call a crisis line, contact your local crisis team, go to emergency care, or call 111 if immediate danger is present.

Self-harm urges: getting through the next wave

What this is for: urges to hurt yourself, punish yourself, feel something when numb, escape shame, relieve pressure, communicate pain, or obey frightening voices.

When not to rely on this alone: if you have injured yourself, taken an overdose, feel unable to stay safe, or may act on urges soon, use urgent support now. In New Zealand call 111 for immediate danger or call/text 1737 for free 24/7 mental health support.

Start with understanding, not judgement

Self-harm can become a powerful way of surviving overwhelming feelings. That does not mean it is safe, and it does not mean you are bad. The aim is to increase safety, reduce immediate access to harm, and build more options over time.

First 10 minutes: lower immediate risk

  • Name the urge: “An urge to self-harm is here.” Naming it can create a small gap between the urge and the next action.
  • Delay: set a 5–10 minute timer. You only need to get through this short window first.
  • Move toward people: go near another person, leave the bathroom/bedroom if needed, sit in a shared space, or message someone: “I am not safe alone right now.”
  • Move away from means: put distance between you and objects, medication, alcohol/drugs, driving, heights, or places that increase risk. Ask someone safe to hold items if appropriate.
  • Use body-based coping: cold water on hands, feet on floor, safe movement, holding ice wrapped in cloth, squeezing a towel, or wrapping in a blanket.
  • Use expression: write uncensored thoughts, draw the feeling, rip scrap paper, cry, or say the feeling out loud to a safe person.

Support ladder

Urge levelNext step
Low to moderateDelay, ground, use a safer sensory action, and write what triggered the urge.
Strong or risingDo not stay alone. Contact a support person, key worker, GP, crisis team, or call/text 1737.
Immediate danger, injury, overdose, or unable to stay safeCall 111, go to emergency care, or ask another person to call emergency help and stay with you.

After the wave

Write down what happened before the urge, what you needed, what helped even slightly, and what made risk worse. This can become part of a crisis plan. If self-harm is repeated, escalating, or linked with voices, intoxication, withdrawal, trauma flooding, or feeling trapped, use professional support.

Try this now: write three safer actions, one person who can be near you, one professional support, and one urgent service you can use before acting on an urge.

If you are supporting someone else: listen, ask directly about safety, stay nearby if risk is immediate, reduce access to means where safe, and get backup. Do not promise secrecy when someone may be unsafe.

NZ support: 111 for immediate danger. Call or text 1737 for free 24/7 mental health support. Manawa — My Own Survival Plan may help some people build a survival plan, but it does not replace urgent help.

Soothing and safety: calming the threat system

What this is for: feeling frightened, ashamed, raw, unsafe, overwhelmed, frozen, or stuck in a threat response.

When not to rely on this alone: use crisis or clinical support if risk is escalating, trauma memories are flooding, voices command harm, substances are involved, or you cannot stay safe.

Why this helps

Soothing is a learned skill. Some people did not have enough safe soothing from others, or trauma taught the body to stay on alert. Soothing practice is not weakness; it is nervous-system care.

Build a soothing menu

  • Touch: blanket, warm shower, comfortable clothing, grounding object, pet, or weighted pressure if safe.
  • Sound: calming music, nature sounds, a familiar voice, karakia/prayer if this fits you, or quiet.
  • Sight: soft light, photos of safe people/places, nature, tidy one small area.
  • Smell/taste: warm drink, mint, soap, safe scent, familiar food.
  • Connection: sit near someone safe, send a simple message, visit a public safe place, or contact support.

Create a small survival kit

Choose 3–5 items: grounding object, photo, note to self, sensory item, music list, crisis numbers, or a reminder of reasons to keep going. Put it somewhere easy to reach.

Try this now: choose one soothing item and one safety contact to add to your kit.

Note: soothing skills take practice and may not work instantly. Praise small attempts; repetition matters.

Mindful pause: noticing without judging

What this is for: stress, autopilot, rumination, emotional reactivity, or needing a short pause before choosing what to do next.

When not to rely on this alone: if internal focus increases panic, dissociation, trauma memories, voices, or paranoia, use eyes-open grounding, movement, or support instead.

What mindfulness means

Mindfulness is paying attention on purpose, in the present moment, without judgement. The mind will wander. The practice is noticing and gently returning, not emptying the mind.

Try a 60-second mindful pause

  • Notice: what thoughts, feelings, body sensations, and surroundings are here?
  • Anchor: choose one safe focus: feet, sound, object, breath, hand movement, or the room.
  • Return: when the mind wanders, say “thinking” and come back to the anchor.
  • Widen: notice the room and ask, “What is one useful next step?”

Adapt it safely

Keep eyes open. Use external objects rather than body scans. Use walking, stretching, colouring, dishes, gardening, or a cup of tea as mindful activities if sitting still is uncomfortable.

Try this now: notice one sound, one colour, one point of contact, and one next step.

Mood early warning signs: noticing shifts sooner

What this is for: mood swings, sudden emotional shifts, high energy, irritability, mixed feelings, risky decisions, withdrawal, or mood changes linked with sleep loss.

When not to rely on this alone: seek professional support early if mood changes include very little sleep without tiredness, racing thoughts, risky behaviour, suicidal thoughts, command voices, paranoia, or feeling out of control.

Why this helps

People often describe mood as “good” or “bad”, but there are many mood states in between. Naming patterns earlier can help you protect sleep, reduce triggers, and involve support before things escalate.

Map your mood state

  • Name it: wired, flat, ashamed, energised, angry, numb, unsafe, restless, hopeful, sad, irritable.
  • Check vulnerability: sleep, food, pain, substances, conflict, trauma reminders, hormones, isolation, medication changes.
  • Notice behaviour: spending, messages, speeding up, withdrawing, arguing, self-harm urges, risk-taking, or avoiding everything.
  • Choose a stabiliser: sleep routine, reduce substances, postpone major decisions, grounding, support contact, routine, or urgent help.

Make an early-warning plan

Write: “When I notice ___, I will ___, and I will contact ___ if it continues.” Share this with trusted people if helpful.

Try this now: write two early warning signs and one action that usually helps you stabilise.

Personal wellbeing plan: one small step in five areas

What this is for: building routine, resilience, connection, and recovery supports without turning self-care into pressure or blame.

When not to rely on this alone: if risk is high, basic needs cannot be met, housing/food/safety are unstable, or symptoms are severe, use practical and professional support as well.

The Five Ways to Wellbeing

  • Connect: talk, listen, text someone safe, sit with whānau, attend a group, or reconnect with community.
  • Be active: walk, stretch, garden, play, move gently, or use a low-energy version.

Coping with voices: reducing distress and increasing choice

What this is for: hearing voices, sounds, or messages that are intrusive, critical, distracting, threatening, comforting, confusing, or more active under stress, sleep loss, isolation, or substance use.

When not to rely on this alone: seek urgent support if voices command harm, you feel unable to stay safe, distress is escalating, sleep has collapsed, substances are involved, or you are frightened you may act on what voices say.

A non-judgemental starting point

Some people hear voices or have unusual sensory experiences. Hearing voices is not always the same for everyone. The first aim is usually not to force the experience to disappear; it is to reduce distress, increase choice, and stay connected with support.

Notice patterns without over-analysing

  • When: time of day, how long it lasted, and whether it followed poor sleep, stress, conflict, boredom, silence, alcohol/drugs, or a trauma reminder.
  • Where: home, outside, bed, shower, bus, work, school, social media, or a high-stimulation place.
  • Voice style: critical, threatening, distracting, comforting, commenting, or commanding.
  • Distress level: 0–10, and whether you felt pulled to do something unsafe.
  • What helped: sound, movement, connection, food, sleep, grounding, medication routine if prescribed, or lower stimulation.

Choose a coping option that fits the moment

  • If the voice is intrusive: try competing sound such as music, radio, a podcast, humming, reading aloud, or headphones in one ear.
  • If silence makes it louder: use structured activity — walking, chores, puzzles, drawing, showering, gardening, gaming, or being near another person.
  • If fear is high: orient to the present by naming the room, date, time, and three ordinary objects around you.
  • If the voice is critical: try a short boundary such as, “I hear you, but I am choosing what I do next.” Stop if this increases distress.
  • If the voice gives unsafe commands: do not debate, test, or negotiate with it. Move toward people, reduce access to means, and contact urgent support.
  • If patterns repeat: protect sleep, food, medication routines if prescribed, connection, low stimulation, and early support contact.

Start with distress and safety

What this is for: a simple plan for noticing early changes before voices, suspiciousness, unusual beliefs, or distress become harder to manage.

Use this when: you have had experiences such as hearing voices, feeling very suspicious, feeling watched or unsafe, or having beliefs that become stronger during stress, sleep loss, substance use, medication changes, or mood changes.

Do not rely on this alone when: voices are telling you to harm yourself or someone else, you feel unable to stay safe, you are not sleeping and feel unusually energised, paranoia is escalating, or other people are worried about immediate risk. Social anxiety: stepping back into life

What this is for: fear of being judged, embarrassed, criticised, watched, rejected, or seen as awkward. Social anxiety can happen before, during, and after social situations.

When not to rely on this alone: use professional support if anxiety is severely limiting life, if alcohol or drugs are being used to cope, if self-harm or suicidal thoughts are present, or if trauma, psychosis, mania, or severe depression are also affecting safety.

Why this helps

Social anxiety is maintained by a loop: threat predictions, body symptoms, self-focused attention, safety behaviours, avoidance, and replaying things afterwards. You do not have to remove anxiety before taking a small step. The goal is to help your brain learn from real experience.

Map the loop

  • Situation: What social situation feels hard?
  • Prediction: What do you fear people will think or do?
  • Body: What sensations show up?
  • Attention: Do you focus on yourself, the other person’s reactions, or the task?
  • Safety behaviours: What do you do to prevent embarrassment?
  • Avoidance or replay: Do you avoid, leave early, or analyse everything afterwards?

One small practice step

Choose a step that is mildly to moderately uncomfortable, not the hardest possible task. Examples: say hello, ask one question, stay in the room five minutes longer, make a short phone call, or attend a group and stay for the first part.

If this does not work

The step may be too big, too vague, or too focused on “performing well.” Shrink the step. Measure what you learned, not whether you felt calm.

Social anxiety experiments: predictions, safety behaviours, and stepladders

What this is for: testing social fears in a planned, gradual way instead of relying only on anxious predictions or avoiding everything that feels risky.

When not to rely on this alone: use support if the practice involves genuine danger, workplace conflict, bullying, discrimination, trauma reminders, intoxication, self-harm risk, or situations where safety planning is needed.

Step 1: make the prediction specific

Instead of “it will go badly,” write the exact fear: “They will laugh,” “I will blush and everyone will notice,” “I will blank out,” or “they will think I am boring.” Rate belief from 0–100.

Step 2: identify safety behaviours

Safety behaviours are things you do to stop the feared outcome, such as avoiding eye contact, rehearsing every sentence, staying silent, over-apologising, checking your phone, hiding symptoms, using alcohol, or leaving early. The same behaviour can be helpful or unhelpful depending on why you use it.

Step 3: plan one experiment

  • What will I do?
  • Where and when?
  • Which safety behaviour will I reduce slightly?
  • What evidence will I look for?
  • How will I support myself afterwards?

Step 4: use a stepladder

List steps from easier to harder. Start with something challenging but doable. Repeat steps as needed. The purpose is learning and confidence, not forcing yourself into the hardest situation.

Step 5: review fairly

Ask: What happened? What did I predict? What evidence did I notice? What did anxiety exaggerate? What is my next small step?

ABCDE thought check

What this is for: slowing down a strong emotional reaction and testing the thought or belief that is driving it.

When to use it: after a specific event where your reaction feels bigger than the facts you can clearly see.

When not to rely on it alone: do not use it as the first step during crisis-level distress, trauma flooding, intoxication, command voices, or immediate danger. Stabilise first.

The five steps

AActivating event: what happened? Describe it like a camera.
BBelief: what did your mind say it meant?
CConsequences: what feelings, body sensations, actions, or urges followed?
DDispute gently: what evidence fits, what does not fit, and what else could be true?

Problem-solving: one real problem, one next action

What this is for: use this when there is a practical problem you can do something about, even if the whole situation cannot be fixed today.

Why this helps

Worry and problem-solving can feel similar, but they are different. Worry often loops around “what if” questions. Problem-solving slows things down, defines one current issue, and turns it into one realistic action.

Alcohol and mood check

What this is for: noticing whether alcohol is affecting mood, anxiety, sleep, relationships, safety, money, work, medication routines, or relapse warning signs.

When to use it: if drinking has increased, other people are worried, alcohol is used to cope, anxiety or depression feels worse the next day, sleep is disrupted, or alcohol is making voices, suspiciousness, impulsivity, or conflict harder to manage.

When not to rely on it alone: if you may be dependent, have withdrawal symptoms, drink heavily most days, mix alcohol with other drugs or medication, are pregnant, feel unsafe, have self-harm thoughts, or have psychosis/mania warning signs. Get professional support before making major changes.

First check: is this safe to change on my own?

Do not suddenly stop heavy daily drinking without medical advice. Alcohol withdrawal can be dangerous. Seek urgent medical help if withdrawal includes confusion, seizures, hallucinations, severe shaking, chest pain, collapse, severe vomiting, or you cannot stay safe.

For non-urgent alcohol or drug support in New Zealand, the Alcohol Drug Helpline is available 24/7 on 0800 787 797, free text 8681, or webchat.

QuestionMy notes

Lower-risk experiment options

  • Track without judging: note drinks, mood before, mood after, sleep, anxiety next day, and any safety concerns for one week.
  • Add friction: buy less, avoid drinking alone if that is risky for you, delay the first drink, slow the pace, or plan an earlier finish.
  • Reduce next-day harm: eat before drinking, alternate with water or non-alcoholic options, plan transport, and avoid driving.
  • Protect relationships and safety: avoid major decisions, conflict conversations, risky messages, spending decisions, or unsafe situations while intoxicated.
  • Bring in support: talk with a GP, pharmacist, prescriber, alcohol and drug service, or trusted support person if medication, withdrawal, pregnancy, psychosis, mania, trauma, family violence, or self-harm risk is relevant.

Alcohol, drugs, and relapse warning signs

Alcohol and other drugs can lower distress in the short term but may worsen sleep, anxiety rebound, mood swings, judgement, medication routines, conflict, and relapse warning signs. Cannabis and stimulant-type drugs can be especially risky for some people who experience voices, paranoia, or psychosis. If symptoms increase after use, treat that as useful information and get support early.

If this does not work

If you cannot cut down, keep drinking more than intended, feel withdrawal, hide drinking, mix substances, or risk is rising, this is not a willpower failure. It is a sign to use extra support. Start with a GP, alcohol and drug service, Alcohol Drug Helpline, mental health team, or trusted support person.

Try this now: write one line: “When I drink, I am usually trying to change _____. The next-day cost is usually _____. One safer step this week is _____.”

Urgent support: if alcohol or drug use is linked with immediate danger, overdose, violence, self-harm, withdrawal, hallucinations, severe confusion, or inability to stay safe, call 111 in New Zealand or seek urgent medical help.

Unsafe relationships: getting support safely

What this is for: use this when a partner, ex-partner, family member, flatmate, carer, or another person makes you feel frightened, controlled, threatened, pressured, monitored, sexually unsafe, or unable to get help freely.

When not to rely on this alone: if there is immediate danger, call 111 in New Zealand. If using this handout, searching online, printing it, or calling someone could make things less safe, close it and use a safer phone, device, webchat, trusted person, or local service if possible.

Why this helps

When another person may harm or control you, the safest next step is often not a direct conversation. A short support plan can help you contact the right service, protect privacy, and think through safety before acting.

Signs this may be unsafe

  • You feel afraid of their reaction or feel you have to change your behaviour to stay safe.
  • They threaten, hit, hurt, humiliate, isolate, stalk, monitor, pressure sexually, or control money, transport, medication, phone access, children, pets, housing, or whānau contact.
  • You feel unable to say no, leave, sleep safely, use your phone freely, or get help without consequences.
  • They check your device, accounts, location, messages, bank activity, social media, or who you talk to.

Try this step by step

  • Check immediate safety: if danger is happening now, call 111 or move toward safer people, a public place, neighbour, or safer room if possible.
  • Protect privacy first: use a safer device or phone if you are being monitored. Consider webchat if calling is unsafe. Be careful with printed handouts, browser history, shared accounts, and notifications.
  • Contact specialist support: you can ask for advice even if you are not ready to leave, do not have proof, or are unsure whether it “counts”.
  • Prepare a simple script: “I am worried about my safety and need help making a plan.”
  • Plan one small next step: safe contact, code word, essential items, safer transport, support appointment, or advice about children, pets, money, housing, or documents.
  • Avoid sudden confrontation if risk could rise: plan with specialist support where possible, especially if the person monitors you, has threatened harm, or reacts dangerously to independence.

  • Women’s Refuge: crisis line 0800 REFUGE / 0800 733 843. Call 111 if urgent.
  • Shine: free 24/7 family violence helpline and webchat. Phone 0508 744 633.
  • Safe to Talk — Kōrero mai, ka ora: free 24/7 confidential sexual harm support. Call 0800 044 334, text 4334, email, or webchat.

Gambling harm: pause, protect, get support

What this is for: use this when gambling is affecting money, mood, sleep, relationships, work, secrecy, shame, safety, or your sense of control.

When not to rely on this alone: use urgent support if gambling is linked with self-harm thoughts, threats, debt pressure, family violence, illegal activity, or feeling unable to stay safe.

Why this helps

Gambling harm often runs on urgency: chase the loss, keep it secret, try one more time, fix it before anyone knows. A pause plan slows the loop and makes it easier to bring in support.

Try this step by step

  • Name the urge: “There is an urge to gamble or chase losses.”
  • Delay: set a 20-minute timer before any next bet.
  • Change location or access: leave the venue, put the phone away, log out, or move cards/cash away if safe and practical.
  • Contact support: tell one trusted person or contact a gambling support service.
  • Write the real cost: money, sleep, mood, secrecy, relationships, work, safety.
  • Choose one recovery action: food, shower, walk, sleep routine, support call, budgeting support, or blocking/exclusion help.

Common difficulties

Shame can make gambling harm harder to talk about. You do not need to wait until things are “bad enough” before getting confidential advice.

NZ support

Gambling Helpline NZ offers free 24/7 support on 0800 654 655 or text 8006. If gambling-related distress is becoming unsafe, call 111 for immediate danger or call/text 1737 for mental health support.

Medication questions: prepare for a safer conversation

What this is for: preparing for a conversation with a GP, psychiatrist, prescriber, pharmacist, or mental health team about mental health medication, side effects, missed doses, stopping worries, medicine changes, or possible mood and behaviour changes.

When not to rely on this alone: get urgent help for overdose, severe allergic reaction, breathing difficulty, chest pain, severe confusion, severe agitation, fainting, possible lithium toxicity, sudden severe mood or behaviour change, or immediate risk to yourself or someone else. Do not stop, restart, double, reduce, or change prescribed medication without professional advice.

Why this helps

Medication conversations can be hard to organise when you are distressed, embarrassed, tired, or unsure what matters. A short written list helps you give clear information and ask safer questions. It also helps your clinician or pharmacist understand timing, benefits, side effects, missed doses, interactions, and what else has changed.

Before the appointment

  • Current medicines: list the name, dose if known, when you take it, and what you understand it is for. Include over-the-counter medicines, supplements, herbal products, alcohol, nicotine, caffeine, or other substances if relevant.
  • What has helped: mood, sleep, anxiety, voices, paranoia, energy, irritability, concentration, pain, side effects, or daily functioning.
  • What worries you: side effects, weight, appetite, movement symptoms, sexual problems, sedation, emotional flattening, sleep, restlessness, driving, missed doses, cost, stigma, pregnancy, breastfeeding, or interactions.
  • Timeline: write down when the medicine started, stopped, or changed; when the problem began; missed doses; and what else changed around the same time.
  • Pattern notes: note whether the issue started after a new medicine, dose change, stopping a medicine, missed doses, smoking/vaping change, alcohol or drug change, illness, stress, or sleep loss.

Four safety checks to include

CheckWhy it mattersQuestion to ask
Stopping or missing dosesSome medicines can cause withdrawal effects, return of symptoms, or relapse if stopped suddenly.“What should I do if I miss a dose, and how should this medicine be reduced if we ever decide to stop?”
Pregnancy or breastfeedingBenefits and risks can change. Untreated mental distress can also carry risk, so decisions need individual advice.“What is safest for me and my pēpi if I am pregnant, planning pregnancy, or breastfeeding?”
Smoking, vaping, caffeine, alcohol, or drugsChanges in these can affect sleep, anxiety, side effects, relapse risk, and sometimes medicine levels.“Could smoking/vaping changes, caffeine, alcohol, or other drugs affect this medicine or how I feel?”
Mood, behaviour, sleep, or thinking changesSome medicines, including non-mental-health medicines, can affect mood, behaviour, sleep, agitation, or confusion.“Could this new symptom be a medicine effect, interaction, or something else that needs review?”

Questions you can ask

  • What benefits should I expect, and how long might they take?
  • Which side effects are common, which should be reviewed, and which need urgent help?
  • What should I do if I miss a dose? Should I ever take an extra dose?
  • If I want to stop or change this medicine, what is the safest plan and who should I contact?
  • What are my options if this medicine is not helping or side effects are hard to manage?
  • Could this medicine, dose change, missed dose, or stopping affect mood, sleep, agitation, confusion, behaviour, or thoughts?
  • Are there driving, alcohol, pregnancy, breastfeeding, smoking/vaping, caffeine, herbal-product, supplement, or interaction issues I should know about?
  • Where can I find reliable NZ medicine information that is readable for consumers?
  • Should this side effect be reported to Medsafe/CARM, and can you help me decide how?

If something feels wrong

Do not wait until the next routine appointment if symptoms feel severe, sudden, frightening, or unsafe. Contact a pharmacist, GP, prescriber, mental health team, Healthline, after-hours service, or emergency service depending on urgency. If there is immediate danger in New Zealand, call 111.

Do not change the dose alone: if a medicine feels unhelpful, embarrassing, expensive, sedating, or hard to take, use that as a reason to seek advice rather than stopping suddenly. This is especially important with antipsychotics, antidepressants, benzodiazepines, mood stabilisers, sleeping tablets, and medicines used during pregnancy or breastfeeding.

If the conversation feels difficult

You can bring a support person, whānau member, advocate, written notes, or a medicines list. You do not have to remember everything in the appointment.

Try this now: write one sentence: “The main thing I need advice about is…” Then write when it started and what changed around that time.

Crisis and urgent help

Crisis Help

If you or someone else is in immediate danger in New Zealand, call 111 now. Kete is not a crisis service and cannot monitor safety.

Use this section when the priority is safety, support, and getting through the next short period. Do not try to solve everything during peak distress.

Choose the safest next step

Start with the level of risk, not with the skill. Grounding and breathing can help, but they are not enough when immediate safety is uncertain.

Immediate danger

Call 111 now. Use emergency help if someone may die, be seriously hurt, cannot stay safe, is violent, has taken an overdose, or needs Police, Fire, or Ambulance.

Might act soon

Do not stay alone with the risk. Move away from means of harm if possible, contact a trusted person, local crisis team, 1737, or emergency services.

Very distressed, not immediate danger

Use one stabilising action, then contact support if distress stays high. Choose one small next step rather than trying to solve the whole problem.

Unsafe relationship or monitored device

Do not make a visible plan on a device someone else can check. Use a safer phone, trusted person, specialist service, or 111 if danger is immediate.

1. Move toward safety

Move away from immediate means of harm if possible. Go near another person, a public area, or a safer room. Ask someone to stay with you if needed.

2. Contact support

Use your crisis plan, contact a trusted person, call your local crisis team if known, or use national support such as 1737 for free call/text support in New Zealand.

3. Use one stabilising action

Feet on floor, cold water, name five things you see, slow your out-breath, hold a grounding object, or reduce stimulation.

If you are supporting someone

Stay calm, listen, and do not leave the person alone if immediate safety is uncertain. It is okay to ask directly about suicide or self-harm. If there is immediate danger in New Zealand, call 111.

Ask clearly

Use simple wording: “Are you thinking about suicide or hurting yourself?” Asking does not put the idea there; it can help people feel less alone.

Reduce immediate risk

Move away from means of harm if possible, stay nearby, and involve trusted whānau, support people, crisis services, or emergency help.

Get backup

Use 1737, local crisis contacts, the person’s plan, GP/mental health team, or 111 if danger is immediate.

Kete is an early-stage educational resource for Aotearoa New Zealand. It is designed to help people find practical mental health skills, printable handouts, crisis guidance, and support links.

Use Kete as one support tool. If safety, medication, diagnosis, withdrawal, family violence, sexual harm, psychosis, severe distress, or suicide risk is involved, use Kete alongside appropriate professional, whānau, peer, or crisis support.

  • Offer general mental health education.
  • Suggest practical coping and therapy-informed skills.
  • Provide printable handouts for reflection, practice, or appointments.
  • Support between-appointment or between-support practice.
  • Support conversations with a trusted support person, GP, counsellor, nurse, peer worker, pharmacist, prescriber, or mental health team.

  • Diagnose, assess, monitor, or treat anyone.

  • Provide personalised medication, risk, legal, relationship-safety, or emergency advice.
  • Collect, store, or protect personal health information.

  1. Start with safety before skills.
  2. Choose one page, one handout, or one tiny step.
  3. Stop or switch to grounding if reading makes distress worse.

Resources deliberately not emphasised

The supporting documents list many apps, including AI chatbots, mood trackers, and broad wellbeing apps. Kete keeps these secondary because the site’s identity is a trusted skills and handout hub, not an app catalogue or AI therapy substitute. Apps may still be useful when chosen carefully with attention to privacy, crisis safety, age range, and clinical fit.
